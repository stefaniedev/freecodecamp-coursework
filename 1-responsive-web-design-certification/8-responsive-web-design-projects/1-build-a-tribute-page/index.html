Dr. Sigmund Freud

The Founding Father of Psychoanalysis

It is said that Sigmund Freud enjoyed smoking twenty cigars a day for the majority of his life.

Highlights of the Life and Theories of Sigmund Freud:

He is known today as an influential thinker in the realms of psychology and medicine.

In 1856, Freud was born in Moravia, but shortly thereafter his family moved to and resided in Vienna.

Freud considered himself a scientist before anything else, and studied biology and then neurology at the University of Vienna.

Freud was initially intrigued with hypnosis, until further experimentation led him to realize that the effects wore off too quickly.

Sigmund Freud died in 1939 as a result of cancer of the jaw. He was survived by his six children.

Freud then came up with the idea that neuroses such as phobias, hysterical paralysis, and paranoia could be eased by talking about the origins of the neuroses, which resulted from deep and traumatic past experiences hidden from consciousness.

This new treatment for neuroses would allow the patient to confront and deal with their past experiences so that they could move on, a technique described in Freud's book Studies in Hysteria.

Freud's next book, The Interpretation of Dreams, is known today as his best work.

In The Interpretation of Dreams, Freud writes of his theory of the unconscious mind, in that dreams are the result of two mental processes: unconscious forces that reveal a wish, and censorship of said wish.

Freud introduces readers to his controversial theory, named the "Oedipus complex," stating that in the psychosexual development process of a human, a child has an unconscious desire for the parent of the opposite gender.

Freud wrote more works such as The Psychopathology of Everyday Life, Three Essays on the Theory of Sexuality, Five Lectures on Psycho-Analysis, and The Ego and the Id.

At first, Freud's theories were not well-received due to the taboo sexual nature of the analyses. Once Freud gave a series of lectures in the United States, his work was taken more seriously.

Freud wrote many more works before his death, in total more than twenty volumes of theoretical works and clinical studies.
